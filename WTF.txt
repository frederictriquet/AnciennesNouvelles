  writing systems – but the combination, the particular encoding, there's no record of it. It's not a cipher. It's a writing
   system, a complete one, but one that no one should have had the knowledge to construct."

  What the Water Remembered              
                                         
  The drought had lasted three years before anyone thought to ask the river.
                                                                                                                            
  Marta had grown up knowing that rivers, in her grandmother's country, were the kind of thing you asked. They had opinions.
   They had preferences. They held grudges in the way water holds the shape of the vessel it was poured from.               
                                                                                                                            
  Here, in this flat midwestern town where she'd lived for thirty years, people did not ask rivers anything. They measured  
  them. They monitored them. They issued flood warnings when the rivers rose and drought emergency declarations when they
  fell. But they did not ask.                                                                                               
                                                                                                                            
  The river that ran east of town was called the Barrow. It had been, Marta understood from historical documents,           
  considerably larger once. A hundred and fifty years of agriculture had changed the landscape in ways that directed water
  elsewhere, and now the Barrow was a meandering thing, uncertain of itself, sometimes no more than shin-deep even in wet   
  years.                                                                                                                    

  This was a very dry year.                                                                                                 
   
  Marta went to the Barrow on a Tuesday afternoon in late July, carrying a thermos of coffee and the knowledge that what she
   was about to do was, by any measure, eccentric. She sat on the bank. The Barrow moved slowly, brownish, a foot or so deep
   at most.                                                                                                                 
                                                                                                                            
  "I know you remember more water," she said.                                                                               
                                         
                                                                                                                            
  It was not a comfortable meeting. She drove home through fields that had been dry for three years, past farmhouses with   
  their windows catching the last light, and she thought about her grandmother's country and the rivers there, and whether
  they remembered anything useful.                                                                                          
                                                                                                                            
  She didn't know if the Barrow had told her anything. She didn't know if rivers told people things. What she knew was that 
  she had gone and sat with it and let herself think about water the way water thinks – not in terms of ownership or rights
